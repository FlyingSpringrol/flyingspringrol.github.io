Creative Technology at Rockwell Group

My work at Rockwell Group

I worked for a year at the Lab At Rockwell Group, building interactive installations. It was an interesting experience for me, and I was pretty out of my comfort zone as far as design went. That being said I had a lot of fun building things for work: for example light installations at Luminaries, VR prototypes for Hardrock Oculus and interactive art for 299 Park Ave.


Idea 1: Realtime VFX compositing for Interactive Installations

I've always been a perfectionist as far as graphics go. I've been obessesed with lighting ever since I started doing graphics work a couple years back, and it's relationship to human perception. I think all people come with some natural algorithms baked into their brains: the ability to detect faces and facial expressions, and also the ability to understand lighting .

The aesthetic difference between those two photos is stark, and highlights why I think studios should lean towards rendered content. The discussions we had at the Lab really came down to trading off pre-computed pieces with realtime interactivity, but I think that was a false dichotomy. I really think there is a potential future in which interactive installations use rendered content in clever ways. For interactive installations there is generally a fixed viewpoint camera: which means in theory you could precompute lighting interactions between layers, and composite in realtime. Just like in Nuke or After effects, using layers to blend layer information could let artists create stunning interactive pre-rendered pieces.

Idea 2: VR as an architectural visualization tool

I've always been a VR fanatic from the first time I put on an Oculus DK2. It truly does feel like a game changer for simulating physical space in the context of architecture. But technology is always adopted on an S-Curve, and we're still sitting at the start of it. For architectural projects in the LAB, whenever a creative technologist is working on them, there will be a VR previz. That being said, it was incredibly difficult to get buy-in from other conventional architecture teams. I think what will cause the exponential technology adoption of VR is every client having a VR headset to view architectural models. Only then will VR will become as necessary as renders. My boss and I got pretty good at building out VR previsualizations for our installations: simulating projection mapping, lights, and unconventional video displays.

Simulating Sensor Data

The problem led to development here was that for most of our installations, depth sensors sit at distances that are hard to mock-up in the office space we had. This idea I thought synergized well with the VR previzes we had already built. Having a library of different simulated sensors could be an amazing thing for developing interactive installations. The example I got around to building was simulating LIDAR data. We also used a lot of depth sensing equipment in our installations, and simulating such data would have been a fun and interesting challenge. Having rich datasets also helped us work in spatial analytics algorithms to the simulations we had built.
